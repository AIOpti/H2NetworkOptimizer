What Is This Platform?

OptiFLO AI is a free, interactive learning platform that teaches you how artificial intelligence can optimize hydrogen networks in oil and gas refineries. No prior AI knowledge needed — everything runs in your browser with zero setup.

In a typical oil refinery, hydrogen is essential — it is used to remove sulfur from fuels, crack heavy oils into lighter products, and meet environmental standards. A refinery may have multiple hydrogen sources (steam methane reformers, electrolysers, by-product streams) and many consumers (hydrocrackers, desulfurization units, hydrotreaters).

The challenge: How do you decide which producers to run, at what rate, to meet all consumer demands at the lowest possible cost? This is called the hydrogen network optimization problem.

This platform lets you solve that problem interactively using a technique called Mixed Integer Programming (MIP) — the same approach used by real refineries worldwide. You can explore pre-built scenarios, build your own networks, and ask an AI tutor to explain every concept along the way.

◈ What is a Hydrogen Network?

A hydrogen network is the system of pipelines, equipment, and controls that produce, purify, and distribute hydrogen across a refinery. Think of it like a city water system: multiple sources feed into a shared grid, and different buildings (consumers) draw from it based on their needs. Each source has a different cost and capacity, and the goal is to supply everyone at minimum cost.

◈ What is Merit Order Dispatch?

Merit order means turning on the cheapest sources first. Imagine you have three generators: one costs $0.01/unit, another $0.04, and a third $0.07. You start with the cheapest and only turn on expensive ones when demand exceeds cheap capacity. This is exactly how the optimizer decides which hydrogen producers to run and in what order.

◈ What is Mixed Integer Programming (MIP)?

MIP is a mathematical optimization technique. "Mixed" means it uses both continuous variables (flow rates that can be any number) and integer/binary variables (ON/OFF decisions). It finds the absolute best solution given all constraints — not just a good guess, but the mathematically optimal answer. Refineries use MIP to save millions of dollars per year in hydrogen costs.

◈ Why Do Purifiers Matter?

Raw hydrogen from producers is not always pure enough for certain consumers. Purifiers like PSA (Pressure Swing Adsorption) and membranes clean it up, but they lose some hydrogen in the process (recovery is typically 85-95%). This means you need to produce MORE than the demand to account for purification losses — a critical factor the optimizer handles.

◈ What Are Constraints?

Constraints are the real-world rules the optimizer must respect: minimum stable production rates (you cannot run a reformer at 5% capacity), maximum capacities, priority levels (the hydrocracker cannot be shut off before a wax unit), and non-curtailability (some consumers must always get full supply). These make the problem hard to solve manually but perfect for MIP.

◈ What Happens During an Outage?

When a major producer goes offline (e.g., an SMR trips), the optimizer must rapidly re-dispatch: ramp up other producers, increase pipeline imports, and if supply is still short, curtail the lowest-priority consumers first. The penalty cost for curtailment incentivizes the solver to avoid it. Try the “SMR-1 Outage” scenario to see this in action.

◈ What Is Pipeline Import?

Import means purchasing hydrogen from an external pipeline that connects to the refinery. Think of it as buying electricity from the grid when your own generators are not enough. It is typically the most expensive source ($0.075/Nm³ in this model) because you are paying a third party. The optimizer only uses import when internal production cannot meet demand — for example, during a producer outage or a demand surge. On the diagram, the orange “Pipeline Import” box shows how much is being purchased and at what cost. If it says “Not needed,” your internal producers are sufficient.

◈ What Is the Export Header?

Export means selling surplus hydrogen out of the refinery through a pipeline to neighbouring facilities. This happens when your producers generate more than your consumers need — often because a minimum-stable-rate constraint forces a producer to run above what is actually required. Rather than wasting this excess, the refinery sells it at a lower price ($0.025/Nm³) to recover some cost. On the diagram, the blue “Export Header” box shows how much surplus is being sold. Export revenue reduces your total operating cost. If it says “No surplus,” all produced hydrogen is consumed internally.

◈ Carbon Footprint & Carbon Intensity

Every hydrogen production method carries a carbon footprint — the total CO₂ emissions attributed to producing each unit of H₂. This is measured as Carbon Intensity (CI) in kg CO₂/Nm³. The optimizer’s dispatch decisions directly determine the network’s carbon footprint: SMR (Steam Methane Reforming) is Scope 1 — direct combustion emitting ~0.27 kg CO₂/Nm³. Electrolysers are Scope 2 — indirect emissions from grid electricity (~0.02 kg CO₂/Nm³ with renewables). By-product H₂ carries an allocated upstream footprint (near-zero for refinery off-gas). The platform calculates CO₂ avoidance: how much carbon is saved compared to an all-SMR baseline. When the optimizer dispatches cheaper SMRs first, CO₂ goes up — this is the cost-vs-carbon trade-off every refinery faces.

◈ Hydrogen Colour Spectrum

Industry classifies hydrogen by its production carbon intensity: Grey H₂ comes from SMR without carbon capture (~10 kg CO₂/kg H₂). Blue H₂ adds carbon capture to SMR (90%+ reduction). Green H₂ uses electrolysis powered by renewables (near-zero emissions). Turquoise H₂ uses methane pyrolysis (solid carbon, no CO₂). The Carbon Intensity gauge in this platform maps your network’s CI from Green (low) through Grey (moderate) to Black (high), helping you visualize where your dispatch strategy sits on this spectrum.
